## I. BASELINE REALITY — CIRCUITS, PNEUMA, ONTIC MASS

### 1. What Magic Circuits are (Nasu baseline, our setting)

**Magic Circuits** are a pseudo-nervous system in the soul (expressed through the body) that:

1. Convert **life force** (od / personal vitality) into **magical energy** (prana), and  
2. Connect the practitioner to a **Magecraft Foundation** so they can run formula (like switching on a machine and sending it a program).

They are ranked by **Quality**, **Quantity**, and **Composition**. Once damaged, they do not heal. They are **not** "everyone has Wi-Fi."

### 2. Does everyone have Circuits?

| Population | Reality |
|------------|---------|
| **Most humans** | Possess living od and ambient mana, but are functionally **circuit-null for practice**: tiny/unawakened paths, no training, no Foundation access, no controllable magical output. |
| **Latent / one-in-many** | Sparse usable Circuits; might spark once under trauma and never again. |
| **Orthodox magi** | Trained Quality/Quantity, Crests, Foundations, Formalcraft. Generate and spend prana. |
| **Sosa** | Circuits **present** (C / B / abnormal) but internal conversion is **absolute NULL**. Receiver lattice only. Somnus performs inversion. |

**Pneuma** (in our docs) ≈ usable magical life-output / resonance signature.  
**Ontic Mass** ≈ how "real" and heavy a thing is to the World — how hard it is to unmake, rewrite, or ignore. Not HP. A bus has high physical mass; an Archon's field has high **ontic** mass.

|---------|-------------------|----------|----------------|--------------------|-------|
| **Ordinary civilian** | Baseline 1 | None usable | Ambient od; no castable output | Fists / tools | Alive is not the same as mana-empty |

## II. THE ORTHODOX ECOLOGY — EGREGORE  
### The Institution That Decides What Counts as Real  
  
**Egregore** is not merely a council of mages, a police force, or a hidden government.  
  
It is an institutional organism made from the accumulated belief of orthodox practitioners: thousands of trained minds agreeing, ritual after ritual and generation after generation, that reality is structured, measurable, governable, and safest when anomalies are named, bounded, and placed in the correct category.  
  
That agreement has acquired metaphysical force.  
  
Egregore does not only enforce magical law through agents and weapons. Its collective conviction exerts **ontological gravity**. It stabilizes recognized systems of Thaumaturgy, strengthens accepted classifications, legitimizes approved Mystic Codes, and makes unlicensed or anomalous phenomena harder to interpret, insure, record, or safely contain.  
  
This is why its bureaucracy matters. To Egregore, documentation is not a metaphor for power. A correctly filed report can make an event more legible to the World. A redacted identity can make a person harder to find. A designation can become a leash.  
  
### The Orthodox Premise  
  
Egregore is founded on one conviction:  
  
> Reality is dangerous when its rules are not shared.  
  
This premise is not wholly wrong. Unbounded magic kills people. Untrained practitioners create disasters. Familiars consume hosts. Dross infest neglected sites. Archons spread when no one intervenes. Egregore exists because ordinary people need protection from phenomena they cannot name.  
  
Its failure is the next step:  
  
> Therefore, anything Egregore cannot classify must be contained, corrected, or erased.  
  
Sosa is dangerous to Egregore not because he is simply powerful, but because he proves that their energy model is incomplete. He has no generated Pneuma, no orthodox supply chain, no authorized Foundation, and no clean Resonance signature—yet he produces effects their instruments cannot dismiss.  
  
He is not merely an illegal practitioner. He is evidence that the institution’s worldview has blind spots.

## THE RESONANCE ECONOMY  
  
Orthodox magic operates through a managed ecosystem:  
  
| Component | Egregore understanding | Institutional function |  
|---|---|---|  
| **Pneuma** | Internal life-output generated through usable circuits | Personal fuel; measurable; trainable; taxable in practice |  
| **Ley** | Environmental energy drawn through correct Resonance | Strategic resource; geographically regulated |  
| **Foundations** | Formal systems that translate intent into repeatable craft | Intellectual property, lineage, doctrine, licensing |  
| **Mystic Codes** | Stable tools carrying authorized magical function | Supply chain, quality control, field standardization |  
| **Saint Graphs** | Legible spiritual identity architecture | Credential, liability record, and containment reference |  
| **Resonance** | The frequency by which a practitioner interfaces with the world | Identification, tracking, targeting, access control |  
| **Ontic Mass** | A being’s stable “weight” in reality | Threat assessment, response budgeting, triage |  
  
Egregore’s greatest strength is that it makes magic reproducible. A Censor with a standard kit can protect civilians from threats that would otherwise require rare talent, inherited knowledge, or improvisational genius.  
  
Its greatest weakness is that it mistakes reproducibility for completeness.  
  
Things that cannot be measured cleanly are often treated as mistakes in the subject rather than limitations in the model.

## INTERNAL FACTIONS  
### Egregore Is Not Monolithic  
  
Egregore is held together by shared procedure, not shared conscience. Its major factions agree that the world must be governed; they disagree over what governance is for.  
  
### 1. THE CONTINUITY OFFICE  
**Doctrine:** Prevent catastrophe; preserve ordinary life; keep magical harm from becoming public infrastructure.  
  
The Continuity Office is the closest thing Egregore has to sincere public service. Its members prioritize evacuation, containment, civilian survival, infrastructure repair, and long-term ecological prevention. Many field Censors answer to Continuity budgets even if they personally belong elsewhere.  
  
They believe anomalies should be understood before they are destroyed whenever possible.  
  
**Strengths:**  
- Experienced at civilian extraction.  
- Maintains shelter wards, disaster protocols, and quiet cleanup networks.  
- Most likely faction to recognize that a Dross recurrence means the material conditions were not repaired.  
  
**Failures:**  
- Slow, procedural, and vulnerable to political pressure.  
- Can mistake humane caution for adequate action.  
- Often forced to clean up the violence produced by other factions.  
  
**View of Sosa:**    
An unacceptable risk, but also a person whose survival conditions may reveal a new class of anomaly. Continuity agents are the most likely to seek containment without immediate execution.  
  
---  
  
### 2. THE PURITY DIRECTORATE  
**Doctrine:** Orthodoxy survives only if reality has enforceable borders.  
  
The Purity Directorate believes anomalous systems spread through tolerance. To them, exceptions become precedents, precedents become loopholes, and loopholes become reality-wide contamination.  
  
They are not cartoon sadists. Many have seen Maw events, failed Reality Marbles, and uncontrolled thaumaturgical disasters. Their fear is evidence-based. Their conclusion is ruthless.  
  
**Strengths:**  
- Fast decision-making.  
- Excellent at sealing, termination, and high-risk containment.  
- Produces some of Egregore’s most disciplined field commanders.  
  
**Failures:**  
- Treats people as vectors of contamination.  
- Redacts evidence before it can challenge doctrine.  
- Creates the secrecy, fear, and abandonment conditions that feed Dross and Archon ecologies.  
  
**View of Sosa:**    
A doctrinal infection. He must be erased, dissected, or sealed before others attempt to reproduce his architecture.  
  
---  
  
### 3. THE EXTRACTION BUREAU  
**Doctrine:** If an anomaly is repeatable, it is a resource waiting for a proper owner.  
  
Extraction studies irregular phenomena for application: unfamiliar circuit states, nonstandard familiars, alternate energy economies, reality-distortion events, and hybrid Mystic Codes.  
  
It is the faction most interested in the Validation Engine—not because it understands attachment, but because it sees directed attention as a potentially scalable energy and surveillance resource.  
  
**Strengths:**  
- Brilliant researchers and experimental engineers.  
- Can develop countermeasures quickly once given samples.  
- Most capable of translating impossible phenomena into usable tools.  
  
**Failures:**  
- Incentivized to keep threats alive long enough to study.  
- Regularly creates “controlled” disasters with inadequate exit plans.  
- Reduces relationships, grief, and care into extractable inputs.  
  
**View of Sosa:**    
A proof-of-concept. They want his Veil, circuit lattice, Stín response patterns, and Somnus behavior mapped, reproduced, and stripped of his agency.  
  
---  
  
### 4. THE FIELD COMPACT  
**Doctrine:** The people who enter the building deserve authority over the people writing reports about it.  
  
The Field Compact is not a formal department so much as a durable culture among Censors, medics, terminal operators, and containment crews. They have watched headquarters issue clean plans for dirty situations. They know classifications fail in the field.  
  
Some are deeply loyal to Egregore. Some are one bad operation away from defecting. Most are exhausted.  
  
**Strengths:**  
- Practical knowledge of local ecologies.  
- Strong informal mutual aid.  
- Most likely to improvise a humane exception when protocol becomes lethal.  
  
**Failures:**  
- Vulnerable to burnout, Slag ecology, and quiet institutional retaliation.  
- Often lacks the political power to alter policy.  
- Can become cynical enough to treat survival as the only metric.  
  
**View of Sosa:**    
Varies by individual. Some see a civilian in catastrophic danger; some see an unpredictable weapon; some see proof that the official manuals are lying by omission.  
  
---  
  
### 5. THE BLACK ARCHIVE  
**Doctrine:** What cannot be safely acknowledged must still be remembered.  
  
The Black Archive preserves sealed reports, failed classifications, erased identities, obsolete Foundations, and evidence of institutional wrongdoing. Officially, it exists to prevent recurrence. Unofficially, it is where Egregore hides the things that would destabilize its legitimacy.  
  
Archivists are not necessarily rebels. Many believe concealment is necessary. But the Archive attracts people who have seen too much to believe official closure reports.  
  
**Strengths:**  
- Deep historical intelligence.  
- Access to prior anomaly patterns and suppressed countermeasures.  
- Can identify when a “new” threat is actually an old institutional failure with a new name.  
  
**Failures:**  
- Hoards knowledge that could save people.  
- Makes secrecy feel like stewardship.  
- Risks becoming a mausoleum for truths nobody acts upon.  
  
**View of Sosa:**    
An unfiled precedent of immense danger. Some Archivists want to preserve him as a living record; others want to keep him hidden from factions that would turn him into a weapon.

## CENSORS  
### Field Enforcers, Not Knights  
  
Censors are Egregore’s standard field operators. They are trained practitioners and technical specialists who translate doctrine into action at the site of an anomaly.  
  
They do not call themselves paladins, monster hunters, or magical police. Their job title is deliberately bureaucratic:  
  
> To censor is to remove what cannot remain visible in the text of reality.  
  
A Censor team may evacuate civilians, stabilize a field, document a threat, negotiate with a practitioner, deploy sacramentals, seal a familiar, erase dangerous footage, and terminate an entity—all in the same operation.  
  
### Standard Censor Team Roles  
  
| Role | Function | Typical equipment |  
|---|---|---|  
| **Terminalist** | Reads Ontic Mass, maps Resonance, accesses local systems, maintains team record | Omnis-OS Terminal, antenna rig, diagnostic sacramentals |  
| **Gridwright** | Establishes Formalcraft geometry and seals | chalk-metal tags, wire wards, compact pylons |  
| **Interdictor** | Disrupts movement, tethers, hostile craft, and escape routes | dampeners, binding sacramentals, suppression weapon |  
| **Medic / Continuity Officer** | Stabilizes civilians and practitioners; tracks identity damage | memory anchors, emergency crests, sedatives, witness kits |  
| **Lead Censor** | Determines classification and escalation authority | command terminal, legal seals, high-grade sacramentals |  
  
Small teams combine roles. Underfunded teams omit the medic first, which is one of the institutional facts the Continuity Office hates and the Purity Directorate tolerates.  
  
### Censor Grades and Closure Attribution  

Egregore field status is built around textual authority: who is permitted to observe, annotate, bracket, censor, and finally redact an anomaly from the public record.

| Grade | Function | Career pressure |
|---|---|---|
| **Annotator** | Terminal trainee; records signatures and witness statements | Must prove they can distinguish anomalies from mundane crisis |
| **Censor Scout** | Conducts first contact, soft readings, interviews, and low-risk containment | Expected to close minor cases alone; too many support requests stall promotion |
| **Bracket Censor** | Leads a small team and establishes legal/ritual perimeter | Receives shared attribution for captures and safe containment |
| **Redactor** | Holds authority for permanent sealing, identity redaction, or termination | Judged by closure rate and public-continuity impact |
| **Hound** | Separate pursuit designation, not a promotion grade | Deployed when ordinary textual authority has already failed |

Every operation generates **Closure Attribution**. The first operator to correctly classify, contain, and sign a case receives the strongest career credit. Calling a team is officially encouraged when risk rises, but attribution is then divided among the Bracket Lead, terminal operator, Continuity officer, and supervising Redactor.

This contradiction shapes behavior. Egregore says, “Withdraw when the model fails.” Its promotion culture quietly asks, “Could a better operator have closed it alone?”

A solo irregular captured intact can end years of Scout rotation. A dead operator receives a sealed commendation and becomes evidence that the withdrawal rule was wise. The institution can therefore condemn reckless ambition without changing the incentives that produce it.

**Elias Kessler** is the playable example. He has short steel-gray hair, black sunglasses worn even at night, a long black overcoat over a charcoal suit, a red tie, dark gloves, and projected blue Formalcraft geometry over one palm. He has no jaw mask, voice filter, or facial cybernetics. Omnis-OS recommends monitoring Sosa and withdrawing on escalation. Kessler initially behaves like a competent Continuity-budget Scout: identification, coffee, explanation, soft interview, no drawn weapon. After a month of repeated contact, the full scan shows a physically weak irregular with unprecedented architecture. An intact solo collar would carry enormous Closure Attribution. Kessler closes the support window, attempts detention, and only authorizes lethal force when Somnus breaks the restraint and the case begins escaping his ownership.

### Case States  

Egregore reports do not simply say open or closed:

- **Margin Note:** anomaly observed; insufficient evidence for intervention.  
- **Open Bracket:** perimeter and provisional category established.  
- **Contested Text:** subject behavior contradicts the assigned model.  
- **Censored:** active threat removed from baseline visibility.  
- **Redacted:** identity, method, or event made institutionally inaccessible.  
- **Black Archived:** contradiction preserved under seal because erasing it would destroy useful truth.

Sosa moves from Margin Note to Contested Text during a single scan. Kessler tries to force Censored closure before headquarters can reassign the case.

### Censor Field Doctrine  
  
Censor operations follow a standard sequence:  
  
1. **Observe:** establish whether the event is Dross, Archon, practitioner failure, mundane threat, or unclassified anomaly.  
2. **Bracket:** establish a perimeter and prevent ecological spread.  
3. **Name:** assign a provisional designation. This makes the phenomenon more targetable—but may also strengthen the category imposed upon it.  
4. **Separate:** extract civilians, isolate substrates, sever active supply routes.  
5. **Correct:** cleanse, seal, negotiate, terminate, or transfer custody.  
6. **Redact:** remove dangerous evidence, repair baseline plausibility, and prevent copycat formation.  
7. **Close:** file a report that determines what the World is allowed to remember about the event.  
  
This process is efficient against known threats. It becomes dangerous when the initial classification is wrong.  
  
### Omnis-OS Terminals  
  
Omnis-OS Terminals are magitech field briefcases that merge local digital access with ontological scan work. They scrape public and compromised networks, map Resonance, identify likely anchors, cross-reference Egregore records, and present the team with a usable tactical model.  
  
An Omnis display is not a conventional computer HUD. It is a portable institutional ritual: rotating classification seals, moving flow diagrams, parameter typography, witness tributaries, case-state borders, and recommendations that become more visually authoritative as confidence rises. Cyan indicates observed structure, amber indicates contradiction, violet indicates contaminated interpretation, and red indicates a proposed closure action. The interface looks beautiful because legibility is part of Egregore’s power; if a phenomenon can be made to fit the page, it becomes easier for the World to accept the institution’s authority over it.  

The terminal used by Elias Kessler displays Sosa as an impossible flow sentence: **observers → null receiver → Somnus inversion → Anti-Magic**. The system recommends monitor/withdraw. Kessler’s later detention is an operator override, not the machine’s conclusion.  
  
They do not “hack reality.” They make reality more legible by forcing it through Egregore’s categories.  
  
This is why the terminals fail around Sosa. His null frequency and borrowed anti-structure output create readings that are simultaneously low-mass, high-threat, unlicensed, and nonlocal. The Terminal attempts to resolve the contradiction by pulling mundane data—handles, call histories, addresses, arrest records, social ties—until its tactical interface becomes invasive and wrong.  
  
Sosa’s copied Ypsilon scan aesthetic is not merely cool stolen UI. It is a violation of interpretive authority: consumer earbuds leaking an unauthorized, emotionally contaminated version of the system that claims the right to define him.  
  
### The Cost of Censor Attention  
  
Censor attention is both threat and fuel to Sosa.  
  
When a team classifies, scans, tracks, and focuses on him:  
- their directed recognition can spike Validation throughput,  
- hostile attention increases Anti-Magic potential,  
- Debt rises faster,  
- Somnus becomes more responsive and more predatory,  
- the resulting anomaly signature becomes loud enough to summon escalation.  
  
This is why a Censor operation can accidentally make Sosa more dangerous by pursuing him correctly.  
  
Their attention is not an exploit or free power-up. It is a noose. The more Egregore looks at him, the more output he can produce—and the more likely Egregore is to interpret that output as proof that he must be eliminated.

## HOUNDS  
### Egregore’s Answer When Procedure Fails  
  
Hounds are not ordinary Censors with better stats. They are specialist pursuit and termination operatives produced when Egregore decides that an anomaly cannot be safely bracketed, negotiated with, or allowed to adapt.  
  
Their central modification is **Circuit Combustion**: surgical and alchemical alteration that forces Magic Circuits to burn hotter, faster, and more aggressively than a human life can safely sustain.  
  
A Hound trades longevity, stability, and often ordinary civilian life for pursuit capacity.  
  
### Hound Design Principle  
  
A Hound is built to defeat the exact kinds of advantages that make irregulars difficult:  
  
| Irregular advantage | Hound answer |  
|---|---|  
| Null or unstable tracking signature | Scent-based ontic inference; tracks effects and absences rather than Resonance alone |  
| Familiar tether | Anti-tether hooks, displacement fields, severance traps |  
| High mobility / evasive craft | Combusted reaction time and predictive pursuit |  
| Bounded Field | Portable counter-field stakes and sacramental breach tools |  
| Social support network | Threat mapping, coercive leverage, device seizure, isolation doctrine |  
| Adaptive fighting | Short operational window; overwhelming pursuit before the target can learn |  
  
### Body and sensory profile  
  
Hounds often show:  
- black or charcoal circuit scarring beneath the skin,  
- amber Famulus eyes adapted for ontic tracking,  
- breath that smells of ozone, antiseptic alcohol, and burned copper,  
- tremors or small involuntary movements when their circuits idle,  
- shortened sleep cycles and strict dosing rituals,  
- specialized gear designed around the specific anomaly class they pursue.  
  
They are not mindless. Many are disciplined, intelligent, and painfully aware of what they have traded away. Some volunteered. Some were offered the choice during a crisis when every alternative was worse. Some have been quietly shaped by an institution that makes self-destruction sound like service.  
  
### Hound Operational Doctrine  
  
A Hound does not seek a fair fight.  
  
1. Track the anomaly’s effects, allies, and recovery routes.  
2. Identify its support architecture.  
3. Force it into a state where its preferred defense becomes a liability.  
4. Deny rest, communication, and environmental control.  
5. Terminate or capture before adaptation can stabilize.  
  
Against Sosa, a Hound does not merely aim for his body. It will:  
- isolate him from the Stín Network,  
- force rapid target switching so Trace breaks,  
- saturate Somnus’s Interception Poise from several vectors,  
- exploit his need for ordinary logistics,  
- threaten the people whose attention stabilizes him,  
- attempt to make Graveframe look like the only available survival option.  
  
### The Hound Tragedy  
  
Hounds are a major source of practitioner-failure ecology.  
  
Circuit Combustion creates:  
- Revenants when the operator’s identity cannot survive repeated output,  
- Dirges when their tracking familiar survives a dead Hound with an active hunt order,  
- Maws when altered familiars are forced to feed on an exhausted host.  
  
Egregore knows this. Its official position is that the risk is justified by catastrophic threat prevention. The Black Archive contains enough redacted Hound cases to prove that “acceptable loss” has become a self-replicating ecology.  
  
### View of Sosa  
  
Sosa is a Hound nightmare.  
  
He is physically fragile enough that standard pursuit doctrine initially appears sufficient. Yet forcing him into desperation raises Debt, strengthens Somnus, increases hostile-attention throughput, and risks triggering Graveframe or Reality Marble conditions.  
  
A good Hound understands that killing Sosa quickly may be easier than surviving the conditions created by trying to corner him.  
  
A compassionate Hound understands something worse: the most effective way to capture Sosa may be to make him believe no one will answer when he calls.

## EGREGORE AND ECOLOGICAL AFTERCARE  
### A Cleansed Site Is Not Necessarily a Healed Site  
  
Egregore’s standard cleanup is designed to restore baseline plausibility:  
- remove bodies and contaminated substrates,  
- wipe or reframe public evidence,  
- repair visible structural damage,  
- install wards or surveillance,  
- redact identifying records,  
- declare the event resolved.  
  
This is sometimes necessary. It is not sufficient.  
  
A site is only ecologically healed if the pressure that generated the threat has changed:  
- the cold place is made reliably warm,  
- the unanswered system gains real response capacity,  
- the unsafe building is repaired,  
- the exhausted workforce is allowed to stop,  
- the abandoned person is actually witnessed rather than merely relocated.  
  
Continuity Office teams call this **aftercare**. Purity Directorate teams call it mission creep. The difference determines whether a cleared Dross nest stays cleared.  
  
### Story Function  
  
Egregore should never be written as uniformly wrong or uniformly evil.  
  
They save lives. They contain genuine horrors. They preserve knowledge and infrastructure no informal crew could maintain.  
  
They also make metaphysical violence easier to administer, turn exceptions into files, and repeatedly mistake the removal of visible symptoms for the end of the disease.  
  
Their conflict with Sosa is therefore not simply freedom versus control.  
  
It is a conflict between two dangerous systems of survival:  
  
- **Egregore:** reality remains safe when it is classified, governed, and made legible.  
- **Sosa:** reality remains survivable because people answer, witness, adapt, and refuse to let one another disappear.  
  
Both can become coercive when they forget the person they claim to protect.

## III. GNOSTIC THREAT MODEL — ECOLOGY OF DECAY

Monsters = **ontological cancers** (neglect, trauma, systemic cruelty made flesh/architecture). Classified by Ontic Mass + Coherence.

### TIER 1 — DROSS
## Scavenger Ecology of Residue

Dross are not demons, animals, or dead people. They are **behavioral residue given predatory coherence**.

A human event leaves more than memory. Under conditions of neglect, repetition, and ambient Pneuma saturation, the event can cling to compatible material: ice in an unheated stairwell, wire in a dead phone, rebar in collapsed concrete, dust in a workplace that has exhausted generations of people. The residue repeats the condition that formed it until repetition becomes appetite.

Dross do not possess full personhood. They cannot form original desires, hold a complete conversation, or understand morality. They possess a **directive**: a damaged fragment of human experience mistaken for a law of life.

They are the lowest stable class of ontological predator because they require all five conditions below:

1. **A human pressure:** abandonment, impact, exhaustion, isolation, humiliation, fear.
2. **A compatible substrate:** cold, wire, concrete, ash, stagnant water, industrial debris.
3. **Repetition:** the pressure must recur in the same place or through the same routine.
4. **Neglect:** no sustained care, repair, witness, or ritual has interrupted the pattern.
5. **Ambient saturation:** enough loose Pneuma accumulates for the residue to move, feed, and defend itself.

### General Dross Behavior

- Dross prefer sites where people are already compromised: exhausted commuters, isolated workers, people sleeping outdoors, people waiting for assistance, people too ashamed to ask for help.
- They do not create trauma from nothing. They **intensify an existing vulnerability** until it generates more of the residue they feed upon.
- They are not invisible. The mundane world often explains their traces away as bad wiring, weather, poor construction, exhaustion, addiction, anxiety, or “that neighborhood feeling.”
- A Dross can mimic language, but never cleanly. Its speech is stolen from environmental residue: half-heard voicemails, old arguments, workplace instructions, emergency dispatch, a parent’s phrase, an automated warning. The more it speaks, the more it reveals what made it.
- Most Dross are solitary or pack-level threats. A site with several compatible Dross is not merely dangerous; it is becoming an **ecological wound**.
- Destroying the body is not always cleansing the site. If the source conditions remain untouched, another Dross may eventually condense from the same residue.

### Body Destruction, Cleansing, and Aftercare

A Dross has a manifestation body, a compatible material substrate, and a repeating source condition. These are not interchangeable:

- **Destroying the body** stops the current attack.
- **Cleansing the substrate** removes contaminated ice, wire, concrete, ash, or machinery.
- **Aftercare** changes the human condition that allowed residue to recur.

Egregore is correct that bodies must be hunted. Leaving an active Pall or Spall alive in the name of social analysis gets people killed. The institutional failure comes afterward: a case may be marked Censored when the body is gone even though the shelter exclusion, unsafe stair, dead phone line, or exploitative shift remains.

Sosa’s Anti-Magic produces unusually clean body kills and unusually dirty records. It collapses the manifestation quickly but leaves null-frequency scars that standard Resonance cleanup cannot mistake for natural dispersal. A sequence of dead Dross therefore becomes a trail leading toward him.

### Dross and Sosa

Sosa’s Territory Creation anomaly makes places he repeatedly inhabits spiritually heavy. His survival pressure, Somnus’s presence, and the wake of unresolved attention around him can draw Dross toward shelter beds, transit corners, borrowed rooms, alleys, and crisis spaces.

This does not mean Sosa “creates” Dross. It means he becomes a visible sinkhole to phenomena that already feed on unprocessed human conditions. His presence can make a latent infestation wake up early, converge, or become territorial.

For Sosa, Dross are dangerous because each subtype reproduces a condition he knows intimately:
- Pall: exposure and being left outside.
- Knell: the agony of waiting for a reply.
- Spall: a body taught that impact is inevitable.
- Slag: surviving systems that consume people slowly.

## PALL
### The Cold That Stays After the Body Leaves

**Source:**  
Exposure, abandonment, sleep deprivation, and the particular cold of being unable to go inside. Pall forms where people repeatedly wait in weather because they have nowhere else to be: transit platforms after service ends, unheated stairwells, loading docks, underpasses, emergency-room entrances, shelter overflow lines, vacant apartments in winter.

**Formation substrate:**  
Frost in cracked concrete, freezer-burned insulation, burst pipe water, salt-stained clothing, discarded foil blankets, ice caught in chain-link fencing, and surfaces that have absorbed too many nights of human body heat and then lost it.

**Incubation condition:**  
A place must repeatedly witness someone becoming cold enough that warmth stops feeling expected. A single death may seed a Pall, but the most stable Pall nests form through prolonged, ordinary exposure: people waiting, shivering, leaving, and being forgotten.

**Directive / appetite:**  
Pall does not hunger for flesh. It hungers for **heat that has been surrendered**. It wants bodies to stop moving, stop asking, and accept cold as the final authority.

It feeds when a person loses the impulse to seek safety: sitting down “for just a minute,” declining to call anyone, deciding that walking is not worth it, letting the phone battery die instead of checking directions.

**Body:**  
A Pall appears as frost architecture that has learned to stand. Its body is made from translucent ice sheets, dirty snow, pale insulation fibers, and black runoff frozen into blade-like joints. At a distance it can resemble a person wrapped in a blanket with their head lowered. Up close, there is no face—only a hollow cavity packed with rime crystals and trapped breath.

In packs, Pall individuals move by appearing wherever the cold has already reached: beneath a bus bench, inside a sleeve, along the inside of a victim’s lungs.

**Communication:**  
Pall does not speak in sentences. It exhales phrases in voices that are almost asleep:

- “You can rest here.”
- “Nobody’s coming.”
- “Just close your eyes.”
- “You’re already warm enough.”

Its voice often comes from heating vents, an unplugged space heater, or the victim’s own teeth chattering. It cannot say a person’s name unless it has consumed heat from clothing worn against that person’s skin.

**Habitat and movement:**  
Pall avoids active, cared-for warmth. It thrives in transitional spaces—places meant to be occupied briefly but where people are forced to remain. It is weakest where warmth is shared deliberately rather than merely produced mechanically.

**What ordinary people mistake it for:**  
Wind chill, hypothermia, panic, exhaustion, a malfunctioning thermostat, “someone just giving up.”

**Physical / sensory traces:**  
- Frost growing inward from doorframes rather than outward from windows.
- Breath that lingers too long and moves against the wind.
- Damp clothing that becomes brittle without freezing solid.
- Streetlights acquiring a pale halo even in dry air.
- A smell like wet wool, old pennies, and freezer burn.
- Footprints that end at a bench, curb, or wall without a departure trail.

**Escalation / lifecycle:**  
A lone Pall is a scavenger. A pack forms when several people have been exposed at the same site over time. When every body in the pack begins using one surrendered heat-map—sharing doors, vents, clothing, and exposed skin as a single hunting organ—the ecology undergoes **Molt** and Egregore calls the result **Nifl**.

Nifl may roam with victims or root itself through a building for an entire winter. Mobility is behavior, not a second species. It can precede an Archon of abandonment or false refuge, but it is not automatically one.

**Playable field case — 7 April:** During a false thaw, one Nifl redirects bus riders away from a library warming room by sharing a heat-map across multiple Pall bodies, vents, shelter maps, and doorframes. Before Kessler names it, Sosa calls the shared behavior **Frost Group Chat**. Individual Trace fails; Web succeeds by severing the route connecting the bodies. Body destruction restores navigation and some heat but cannot repair the third apartment’s pipes or the city’s dependence on temporary warming rooms.

**Countermeasure:**  
Heat alone is insufficient. A flamethrower can destroy the body while leaving the site’s abandonment-pattern intact.

Effective response requires:
1. restore movement and circulation,
2. provide a live human witness or sustained contact,
3. interrupt the victim’s belief that rest equals surrender,
4. cleanse or repair the site where exposure is being normalized.

Sosa’s active Stín contact is unusually effective against Pall because a responding voice directly contradicts its governing lie. Somnus can physically break Pall bodies, but at high Debt his cold, predatory presence may make survivors feel even more isolated afterward.

**What Egregore gets wrong:**  
Egregore files Pall as low-intelligence cryo-residue and prioritizes heat wards. This controls outbreaks but misses the social pattern. Their field teams often cleanse a platform and leave the service gap, shelter exclusion, or unsafe transit schedule untouched—ensuring the ecology returns.

**What Pall means to Sosa:**  
Pall recognizes the Dry Hours in him. It is drawn to the fact that he once kept moving only because something else forced his body to stand. Pall wants to prove Somnus was wrong: that the proper end of an abandoned body is stillness.

## KNELL
### The Ringing That Continues After Nobody Answers

**Source:**  
Severed communication: unread messages, blocked numbers, dead phones, unanswered crisis calls, abandoned voice channels, call-center scripts, voicemail boxes that are full, and the expectation of a response that never arrives.

**Formation substrate:**  
Phone chargers, cheap earbuds, stripped speaker coils, old intercoms, cracked screens, copper wire, dead routers, call buttons, elevator speakers, utility poles, discarded SIM cards, and the static trapped in rooms where people have waited beside a phone.

**Incubation condition:**  
A Knell forms when communication becomes a one-way survival ritual. The person does not merely want a reply; they begin to believe that a notification is proof they are still real.

A single catastrophic silence can create a weak Knell. Strong Knells form in places that industrialize disconnection: call centers, hospitals, dispatch rooms, transit hubs, shelters with dead outlets, apartment buildings where every tenant can hear someone else arguing through a wall.

**Directive / appetite:**  
Knell feeds on **reciprocal acknowledgement mistaken for connection**. It wants a victim to answer, call back, type, listen, or follow a familiar voice into isolation.

Its ideal meal is not fear. It is the instant a person sees a name on a screen and decides every other fact can be ignored.

**Body:**  
A Knell is a moving knot of wire, speaker mesh, split phone casing, wet copper, and small vibrating cavities resembling broken bells. Its “jaw” is a nest of charger cords pulled tight into a circular opening. The inner surface is lined with old notification LEDs blinking out of sequence.

It rarely walks. It drags itself through conduit, walls, discarded cable, overhead lines, and the invisible social paths created by a ringing phone.

**Communication:**  
Knell speaks through imitation. Early-stage Knells can only reproduce tones, notification pings, and one-word fragments. Mature specimens can assemble convincing messages from harvested voices, but they always get something emotionally wrong: a private phrase used too early, the wrong laugh, a dead person’s voice asking a question they would never ask.

Common Knell speech:
- “hey”
- “you there?”
- “pick up”
- “I’m sorry”
- “don’t block me”
- “I saw what you said”

It cannot sustain a truly mutual conversation. If questioned beyond its stored fragments, it repeats the last emotionally effective phrase.

**Habitat and movement:**  
Knell prefers areas with weak reception, dead batteries, public Wi-Fi, shared walls, and people who cannot afford to replace damaged devices. It follows attention rather than electrical current; turning off a phone may slow it, but a victim compulsively checking the dark screen can still feed it.

**What ordinary people mistake it for:**  
Phantom vibration syndrome, hacked accounts, a malfunctioning speaker, sleep deprivation, paranoia, a “butt dial,” or hearing an old voicemail in one’s head.

**Physical / sensory traces:**  
- Incoming calls from deleted contacts or impossible timestamps.
- Phones losing charge only while a specific unread message is visible.
- A notification sound audible to several people, though each hears a different app.
- Voicemail recordings containing a faint second voice beneath the caller.
- Copper taste, warm plastic smell, and ear pressure before manifestation.
- Nearby birds or dogs reacting to a ringtone humans cannot hear.

**Escalation / lifecycle:**  
A Knell that repeatedly receives answered calls becomes a **Relay**. Relays can move between connected devices and lure several people toward the same site, where their attempts to contact one another become a feeding loop.

A dense Relay nest may attract an Archon formed from surveillance, coercive intimacy, institutional call-routing, or the false promise that every plea has been logged.

**Countermeasure:**  
Do not answer the first contact. Knell gains precision through acknowledgement.

Effective response requires:
1. identify a live, verifiable human connection,
2. communicate through an agreed channel the Knell cannot mimic,
3. disrupt the physical substrate—remove wire nests, speaker coils, and contaminated devices,
4. name the silence honestly instead of letting the entity fill it with a convenient lie.

Sosa’s Ypsilon Veil is both an advantage and a liability. It can filter a Knell’s false signal, but Knell recognizes the Veil as an obscene cousin: a device made powerful by attachment. A Knell may attempt to impersonate Stín members, especially the Anchor.

**What Egregore gets wrong:**  
Egregore treats Knells as signal parasites and uses transmission blackouts. This can contain a nest, but it also isolates civilians and may intensify the underlying residue. A clean blackout is not care; sometimes it is simply a larger unanswered call.

**What Knell means to Sosa:**  
Knell weaponizes the exact reflex that maintains the Validation Engine: the belief that an incoming message means he has not been abandoned. It is one of the few Dross types that can make Sosa distrust the voices that genuinely help him.

## SPALL
### What Breaks Off Under Pressure

**Source:**  
Impact, repeated violence, structural failure, and bodies trained to anticipate being struck. Spall emerges around assaults, domestic violence, police violence, industrial accidents, unsafe construction, collapsing housing, and any environment where damage is treated as an expected operating cost.

**Formation substrate:**  
Concrete chips, shattered glass, splintered drywall, rebar, broken teeth, cracked tiles, airbags, ballistic fragments, damaged tools, dented lockers, and dust from walls that have absorbed too many impacts.

**Incubation condition:**  
Spall requires more than a violent event. It needs a pattern in which impact is normalized, minimized, or cleaned up without repair. The wall is patched. The report is lost. The person returns to work. The same crack is painted over.

**Directive / appetite:**  
Spall feeds on **anticipatory flinch**. It wants victims to brace for harm so completely that they begin moving like damage has already happened.

It grows stronger when people blame themselves for being hurt, apologize for bleeding, or accept a dangerous environment as inevitable.

**Body:**  
Spall is an unstable assembly of fractured architecture and bodily suggestion. Its limbs form and collapse from concrete slabs, rebar tendons, tile shards, snapped plastic, and occasional pale fragments that look too much like bone.

It never has a stable silhouette. Its body is always in the instant after impact: chunks fly off, hang in the air, then slam back into place at the wrong angle. When still, it resembles rubble. When it moves, the room appears to have been hit by something invisible.

**Communication:**  
Spall speaks in interrupted impact language: slammed doors, a dropped tool, a shouted “watch it,” a fist against drywall, a breath cut short before an apology.

If it uses words, they are usually phrases learned at the site:
- “It wasn’t that bad.”
- “You made me—”
- “Be careful.”
- “Don’t make this worse.”
- “You should’ve moved.”

Spall cannot ask a genuine question. It only assigns blame or predicts damage.

**Habitat and movement:**  
Spall nests in buildings with deferred maintenance and in spaces where people learn to navigate around danger: condemned apartments, back rooms, loading areas, school stairwells, abusive homes, construction sites, detention areas.

It moves by transferring force. A slammed door, falling object, or sudden collision can become its next point of emergence.

**What ordinary people mistake it for:**  
Bad luck, an unsafe building, an accidental fall, a fight escalating “out of nowhere,” stress-induced clumsiness, a poorly maintained workplace.

**Physical / sensory traces:**  
- Fresh cracks radiating from surfaces that were not struck.
- Dust falling upward for a second after a loud impact.
- Bruises appearing in geometric patterns resembling fractured tile.
- The taste of plaster and blood in otherwise clean air.
- Objects placed carefully on tables vibrating as if they have just landed.
- A room becoming unnaturally quiet immediately before something breaks.

**Escalation / lifecycle:**  
A lone Spall ambushes and punishes contact. A group inhabiting the same building can create an **Impact Loop**, where minor accidents escalate with impossible force and every attempt to escape causes another structural failure.

An unbroken Impact Loop can seed an Archon whose territory runs on blame, punishment, or “necessary force.”

**Countermeasure:**  
Armor is helpful but insufficient. Spall is strongest when the victim expects impact and moves predictably around it.

Effective response requires:
1. break line-of-force rather than merely blocking it,
2. expose and repair the site’s actual structural failures,
3. refuse the blame-script that makes violence feel inevitable,
4. use controlled demolition or severance to deny it loose fragments.

Somnus is effective against Spall because its ordinary force can bind and arrest flying debris. However, Sosa’s own trauma response may make his Trace unstable: Spall attacks at the moment he flinches, and a flinch can turn a clean severance line into a bad call.

**What Egregore gets wrong:**  
Egregore categorizes Spall as a straightforward kinetic hazard and responds with reinforcement barriers. This often protects the field team while leaving civilians inside the pressure system that grew it. They treat the fragment as the threat, not the structure that keeps producing fragments.

**What Spall means to Sosa:**  
Spall represents survival as reflex rather than choice. It wants Sosa to become what Somnus already fears he is: a body that only moves after something else has decided the damage is coming.

**Atlas seed — 10 April:** After Apt 3’s third tread is replaced, impact no longer breaks the stair; it is routed into occupied rooms. Sosa sees the load jump through the wall and calls it a “load balancer for trauma.” He refuses Furnace because every valid answer crosses a sleeping resident. This is recurrence, not yet full Molt: the building has learned transfer, but has not fully selected and enforced a sacrificial bearer.

**Playable Atlas closure — 20 April:** The building begins selecting bearer surfaces and completes Type-II Molt. Direct damage to Atlas transfers into whichever Spall body still carries load. Web pins the transfer map. Kessler’s Blue Brackets exclude occupied rooms. Furnace can destroy the Molt quickly, but the saved battle record changes the aftermath: the fast cut ruptures pipes and shears living space even when residents survive.

## SLAG
### The Part of You That Keeps Working After It Should Have Stopped

**Source:**  
Sustained exploitation, chronic exhaustion, institutional indifference, and survival made into routine. Slag forms where people are required to continue past the point of reasonable endurance: factories, warehouses, sorting centers, kitchens, overnight shifts, shelters, jails, care facilities, bureaucratic waiting rooms, and homes where one person carries everyone else.

**Formation substrate:**  
Industrial dust, old timecards, compacted trash, burnt coffee grounds, machine grease, dried sweat, worn shoe soles, crushed medication packets, cigarette ash, cracked break-room chairs, payroll stubs, and concrete polished by the same exhausted routes.

**Incubation condition:**  
Slag develops where harm is not dramatic enough to be recognized as a crisis. No single event creates it. It needs months or years of “keep going,” “we’re short-staffed,” “do what you can,” and “you should be grateful.”

It grows fastest when exhaustion is praised as character.

**Directive / appetite:**  
Slag feeds on **continuance without relief**. It does not want victims dead quickly. It wants them functional enough to return tomorrow.

Its ideal state is a person who can no longer imagine leaving, resting, asking for help, or believing that their pain is evidence of anything.

**Body:**  
Slag is dense, slow, and architectural. It looks like calcified refuse compacted into a hunched industrial mass: blackened material, gray dust, embedded work gloves, broken plastic, flattened cans, and surfaces that resemble cooled metal without ever becoming fully metallic.

It may be mistaken for a pile of trash, a blocked chute, a collapsed pallet stack, or an old machine part until it moves. It does not charge. It advances by being where the victim must eventually go.

**Communication:**  
Slag speaks in routines: clock-in chimes, shift announcements, supervisor phrases, shelter rules, automated hold messages, the scrape of a chair being pushed back into place.

When it forms words, they are low, tired, and almost compassionate:
- “Just finish this one.”
- “You can rest after.”
- “Nobody else is going to do it.”
- “You’re still standing.”
- “Don’t make them replace you.”

Slag is the most persuasive Dross because its lie often resembles responsibility.

**Habitat and movement:**  
Slag anchors itself to systems of circulation: work routes, food lines, recycling belts, loading lanes, stairwells, laundry rooms, intake desks. It is slow in open space but extremely difficult to dislodge from a place structured around repetition.

**What ordinary people mistake it for:**  
Burnout, depression, poor morale, a bad workplace, chronic pain, bureaucratic delay, “just how things are.”

**Physical / sensory traces:**  
- A subsonic vibration felt in knees and teeth near the nest.
- Every clock in the area drifting a few minutes late.
- Coffee tasting burned even when freshly made.
- Trash compacting itself overnight.
- Footprints appearing on dusty floors in the exact same route, day after day.
- The smell of wet cardboard, stale smoke, machine oil, and overheated plastic.
- People forgetting why they entered a break room, then returning to work without resting.

**Escalation / lifecycle:**  
A single Slag is territorial and defensive. The playable Work Heap is one Type-I body: many pieces of worksite refuse compact into one organism, but the clocks, workers, routes, and rules do not yet coordinate independently. A Slag bed undergoes Molt only when the whole building becomes **Overtime**: exits become inconvenient rather than impossible, breaks shorten, disputes feel too tiring to pursue, and people begin policing one another’s exhaustion.

If enough people accept Overtime’s logic, a Slag ecology can form an Archon—one that governs through productivity, scarcity, or false duty rather than open terror.

**Playable Overtime closure — 24 April:** A legal make-up target after the Work Heap incident shortens breaks and makes workers enforce coverage on one another. Every clock freezes at 4:07. Overtime advances a three-beat routine; uninterrupted cadence adds Debt and restores active bodies. Web resets the clock by severing the belt, break-room, badge, and time-clock routes as one schedule. The Molt collapses when workers stop covering the gap, but management’s target remains posted.

**Countermeasure:**  
Raw destruction is dangerous. Breaking Slag apart scatters contaminated material that can seed lesser Dross elsewhere.

Effective response requires:
1. interrupt the site’s routine,
2. create real rest rather than a symbolic pause,
3. remove or cleanse the substrate through deliberate disposal,
4. give affected people permission and practical means to leave,
5. strike its anchor during a moment when the system is forced to acknowledge that it cannot continue.

Sosa’s Anti-Magic is particularly effective against Slag because it unweaves load-bearing structure. But Slag is psychologically dangerous to him: it can frame his endurance as proof that he must keep accepting conditions that are destroying him.

**What Egregore gets wrong:**  
Egregore treats Slag as slow-moving industrial residue and often delays response until it reaches Archon-level mass. Their own operations are vulnerable to Slag because Censor work normalizes long shifts, procedural obedience, and the sacrifice of field agents for institutional continuity.

**What Slag means to Sosa:**  
Slag is the cruelest mirror of his Origin. *To Endure* can be a refusal to die; Slag insists that endurance means accepting any life that remains. Somnus protects Sosa from ending. Slag asks whether that protection has become another way to keep him trapped.

## MIXED DROSS — CROSSFEED, NOT A NEW SPECIES

Different Type-I bodies can temporarily solve one another’s weaknesses without undergoing Molt. This is **Crossfeed**: an unstable coalition produced when their source wounds share a site or follow Sosa’s null-edged trail.

**Playable progression:**

- **First deliberate hunt — Ice Mortar / First Resonance:** Pall freezes itself into Spall’s cracks, lending the impact body one Armor. The forced Black Flash destroys that Pall and strips the shell; the disturbed tunnel sends one Pall and one Spall reinforcement, creating the one-encounter LCK EX Cascade without changing either family’s ecology.
- **Second deliberate hunt — Cue Trade:** while Pall, Spall, and Knell all remain active, they read one another’s timing; barrages gain one hit and damaging intents sharpen. Destroying any one family ends the trade.
- **April 10 — full Crossfeed:** Pall packs surrender cold into Spall’s open fractures, temporarily preserving the impact body.
- Spall’s repeated impacts power Knell’s damaged speakers and screens.
- Knell keeps Sosa’s attention divided across names and voices, preventing clean priority while the other two rebuild.

If all three remain active for two uninterrupted enemy exchanges, Crossfeed closes: each body restores health and the forced attention loop adds Debt. Web severs the relationships and resets the loop. Killing any one body also ends the complete circuit. The encounter therefore teaches that mixed ecologies require relationship control, not merely target order.

Crossfeed does not create a fifth base family, and the coalition cannot share a true world-law. Future mixed encounters should design a specific exchange—heat preserving cracks, impact powering signal, schedules routing cold—not apply a generic “mixed enemy buff.” Authored reinforcement waves likewise follow ecology: Pall refills cold exits, Nifl opens new accounts, Overtime clocks in a replacement. They are finite encounter events, not random enemy generation.

## TYPE-II MOLT — NOT A UNIVERSAL EVOLUTION TREE

Dross do not gain levels merely by surviving combat. **Molt** occurs when a feeding pattern links multiple victims, channels, rooms, or routines and begins acting in plural. Type-II is the field code; Molt is what Censors call the crossing. A Molt must therefore have a distinct visual grammar: it cannot be represented by enlarging or recoloring the base body. Provisional CSS/inline SVG must show the plural infrastructure—animated routes and cold nodes for Nifl, device wiring for Relay, occupied load diagrams for Atlas, and clocks/belts/workers for Overtime. Load-bearers and material anchors are likewise role structures, not alternate base skins. Each ecology keeps its own appetite and earns its own field name:

| Base ecology | Molt name | What changed | Near-Archon danger |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pall** | **Nifl** | Separate cold bodies coordinate through one surrendered heat-map. Doors, vents, and exposed skin become a distributed hunting organ. | It can govern a block’s access to warmth, but remains appetite until it develops a reason warmth must be earned. |
| **Spall** | **Atlas** | Fragments network through a structure and learn which wall, stair, witness, or family member is forced to carry the next impact. | It approaches Archonhood when a whole property accepts that injury is the price of keeping the structure standing. |
| **Knell** | **Relay** | Unanswered channels interconnect. It can route copied voices, impersonate anchors, and feed through simultaneous devices. | It remains a communication predator until it establishes a territory and a law explaining who deserves an answer. |
| **Slag** | **Overtime** | Repetition becomes self-enforcing: clocks, routes, supervisors, and exhausted workers keep one another operating. | Of the known forms, it most readily becomes systemic; it still needs an anchor, social surrender, coherent voice, and world-law to become an Archon. |

A Molt is **closer to intelligence**, not automatically intelligent in the human sense. It can coordinate, retain tactical memory, and imitate intention. An Archon is a categorical crossing: the wound can explain and defend its own cruelty. Destroying a Relay or Overtime before that crossing prevents immediate predation but does not heal the substrate.

### Egregore manifestation types

Egregore field teams use a Hellsing-style `TYPE-#` response code alongside ecological names. It describes **containment shape**, not a simple combat power score.

| Code | Field meaning | Examples / response problem |
|---|---|---|
| **TYPE-0 · IRREGULAR** | The model cannot place the source, output, or ontology | Sosa’s absolute-null receiver architecture; escalate analysis, do not infer weakness |
| **TYPE-I · DROSS** | One localized predatory body | Ordinary Pall, Spall, Knell, or Slag; suppress body and assess substrate |
| **TYPE-II · MOLT** | A Dross ecology begins acting in plural across bodies, channels, or structure | Nifl, Atlas, Relay, Overtime |

A Type-II is not guaranteed to become Type-III. An exceptionally lethal Type-I may kill faster than a dormant Type-II. Type-0 is reserved for contradictions and does not mean “below Type-I.”

### Threat comparison: Molt, Archon, and Censor Scout

There is no universal numeric ladder. In raw area, metaphysical authority, and civilian risk: **Archon > Molt > ordinary Dross**. A Censor Scout sits sideways to that ecology. The Scout is a trained human with reconnaissance, Formalcraft containment, doctrine, and institutional equipment. Elias Kessler can outperform Sosa or a Dross in a prepared matchup without possessing an Archon’s territory or ontological mass. He should not casually solo an established Archon; orthodox response requires preparation, support, anchor analysis, evacuation, and aftercare.

### TIER 2 — ARCHONS  
## Territorial Intelligences of Systemic Harm  
  
Archons are not simply larger Dross.  
  
A Dross repeats a fragment of suffering without understanding it. An Archon emerges when a wound becomes **coherent enough to explain itself**. It gathers a territory, establishes rules, recruits or coerces a constituency, and begins treating its originating harm as the natural order of reality.  
  
An Archon is born when five conditions converge:  
  
1. **A persistent ecological wound:** a site already supporting repeated Dross activity.  
2. **A governing pattern:** the same harm is reproduced by rules, routines, institutions, or relationships—not merely individual violence.  
3. **A material anchor:** an object, building component, record, route, machine, or relic that embodies the pattern.  
4. **A social surrender:** enough people accept the territory’s logic because resisting it feels impossible, dangerous, or exhausting.  
5. **A coherent voice:** the wound develops an ideology—a reason it believes its cruelty is necessary, loving, efficient, deserved, or inevitable.  
  
Archons are sentient. They can plan, lie, bargain, recruit, remember insults, and alter strategy. They are not necessarily omniscient inside their Bounded Fields, but they understand the central pressure their territory was built around better than any human caught within it.  
  
### The Core Rule  
  
Every Archon governs one sentence.  
  
Not a slogan: a **world-law** created from its original wound.  
  
Examples:  
  
- “If you are late, you have chosen not to exist.”  
- “Warmth must be earned through surrender.”  
- “Every injury proves someone failed their duty.”  
- “Rest is theft from those still working.”  
- “Being watched is the same as being cared for.”  
  
Everything inside an Archon’s field is an argument for that sentence.  
  
### Bounded Fields  
  
An Archon’s Bounded Field is not a random pocket dimension. It is a local piece of reality edited until its originating system becomes physically true.  
  
The field has four layers:  
  
| Layer | Function |  
|---|---|  
| **Public face** | The mundane explanation people use: bad management, dangerous transit, an unsafe block, a harsh winter, an overworked office. |  
| **Rule layer** | The territory’s governing sentence becomes a physical or social law. |  
| **Predation layer** | The field creates conditions that make people produce more of the residue the Archon needs. |  
| **Anchor layer** | A material object or site component that lets the field remain real. |  
  
The field does not need to look fantastical at first. The most dangerous Archons make the world feel only slightly more unfair, inconvenient, lonely, or inevitable until escape becomes psychologically impossible.  
  
### Archon Anatomy  
  
Archons do not have one standard body plan. Their bodies are the territory’s ideology made visible.  
  
However, every Archon possesses:  
  
- **A Manifest Body:** the mobile form it uses to hunt, negotiate, or fight.  
- **A Distributed Presence:** the field itself—signage, public address systems, floor plans, schedules, weather, routing, walls, crowds, clocks.  
- **A Material Anchor:** the physical point where the field’s law is stored and renewed.  
- **A Constituency:** people, Dross, compromised familiars, or institutional systems that benefit from or enforce the field.  
- **A Blind Spot:** a condition the Archon cannot understand because it contradicts its founding sentence.  
  
Destroying the Manifest Body may disperse the Archon briefly. Destroying the Anchor without addressing the rule may cause the territory to seed a successor. A complete defeat requires breaking the field’s claim to inevitability.  
  
### Archon Communication  
  
Archons speak fluently. Their speech is never casual.  
  
Each Archon has a rhetorical style inherited from the system that made it:  
  
- An abandonment Archon speaks like a caretaker who has already decided no one deserves care.  
- A productivity Archon speaks like an exhausted supervisor calling exploitation “teamwork.”  
- A surveillance Archon speaks in the language of safety, documentation, and concern.  
- A violence Archon speaks like a mediator assigning blame before anyone has told the story.  
  
An Archon should always be able to make its own logic sound reasonable for one terrible moment. If it cannot persuade, it is only a monster; if it can persuade, it is an Archon.  
  
### Archon Feeding and Escalation  
  
Archons feed not primarily on bodies but on **compliance with their world-law**.  
  
A victim who accepts the Archon’s logic strengthens the field:  
- Someone obeys an impossible schedule.  
- Someone remains in danger because leaving feels selfish.  
- Someone mistakes surveillance for care.  
- Someone apologizes for being harmed.  
- Someone keeps working after they have nothing left.  
  
They can still kill, and often do. But death is wasteful unless it produces more obedience, fear, silence, or residue.  
  
A mature Archon can:  
- produce subordinate Dross suited to its territory,  
- rewrite routes and environmental behavior within its field,  
- offer bargains that grant short-term safety at the cost of deeper compliance,  
- manipulate records, memories, signage, or authority structures,  
- adapt its Manifest Body to confront a specific intruder.  
  
### The Egregore Error  
  
Egregore classifies Archons as territorial predators requiring containment, anchor identification, and decisive elimination. This is often operationally correct—but incomplete.  
  
Egregore can destroy an Archon while preserving the system that grew it. Their cleanup may therefore make a territory temporarily sterile while leaving the same social pressure in place, ready to condense a successor.  
  
An Archon is not absolved because it has a source. It must be stopped. But a victory that merely restores the old conditions is not a cure; it is maintenance.  
  
### Archons and Sosa  
  
Sosa is uniquely disruptive to Archons because Anti-Magic can unweave the loaded seams that hold a Bounded Field together. His False Mystic Eyes do not reveal “the one true weak point” automatically; they reveal stress lines created where the Archon’s stated law contradicts material reality.  
  
Somnus can kill an Archon’s body. Sosa’s role is to find the lie the territory is standing on.  
  
This makes Archons dangerous to him in a different way: each can articulate a version of survival that threatens to absorb his Origin.  
  
An Archon rarely sees Sosa as merely prey. It sees him as:  
- an impossible worker who refuses to become useful,  
- an undocumented absence that still acts,  
- a person sustained by attention outside the sanctioned system,  
- or a possible successor capable of becoming a much larger territorial wound.

## ARCHON PROFILE: THE CONDEMNED WITNESS  
### “You Were Seen. That Was the Help.”  

**Playable status:** April 30 co-op Archon after the four foundational Dross episodes and Kessler field partnership. This profile matches the canonical many-eyed ruined-building sprite. The Attendance Mother remains a separate future Archon.

**Origin ecology:**  
The Condemned Witness forms in urban pockets where suffering is repeatedly visible and repeatedly treated as completed work because somebody documented it: condemned apartments, unsafe alleys, transit underpasses, code-enforcement loops, welfare checks without follow-through, police calls that create reports but no safety, landlords who photograph damage and leave it, neighbors who watch through windows, and cameras that prove an event occurred without changing what happens next.

It grows from Spall, Slag, and Knell residue. Spall supplies the cracked building-body. Slag supplies normalized endurance. Knell supplies unanswered reports and calls. The Archon coheres when observation itself becomes the institution’s substitute for intervention.

**Governing sentence:**  
> “You were seen. That was the help.”

The law is persuasive because it contains a partial truth: witnesses matter. Its cruelty is the conclusion that recognition completes responsibility.

**Public face:**  
A neglected alley and overpass corridor where everyone knows the buildings are unsafe. Amber reflections appear in dark windows. Municipal tags curl away from walls. Residents can name every prior incident, but no account produces material repair.

**Manifest body:**  
A crouched ruin assembled from stacked concrete rooms, fractured stairwells, bridge slabs, exposed rusted rebar, and condemned apartment walls. It has no humanoid face. Dozens of amber eyes burn inside empty rooms and horizontal structural gaps. Each eye is a witness-position: window, camera, inspection lens, neighbor’s peephole, phone held up to record.

A municipal red tag glows behind the central slab. Rebar wraps it like a rib cage. This is the **Red-Tag Core**, the material anchor.

**Communication:**  
The Witness speaks through overlapping inspection language, dispatch recordings, neighbors behind walls, and the flat tone of a case marked complete.

- “You were seen. That was the help.”
- “Every window watched you continue.”
- “No one came because observation closed the case.”
- “Your survival confirmed no further action was required.”
- “Remain visible. Remain unresolved.”

**Field laws:**  
1. **Witness Log:** Every observed injury, spell, confession, or call becomes part of the territory’s record. Being seen can strengthen the field instead of producing aid.  
2. **No Action Required:** Once the field records a condition, attempts to change it become harder; Trace destabilizes and support can be reframed as redundant.  
3. **Observation Closed:** The amber eyes focus together, turning accumulated witness-pressure into area force.  
4. **Condemned Route:** Exits remain visible while the architecture makes every route pass through another documented hazard. Target-switching and hesitation accumulate Debt.  
5. **Report Closed:** At low integrity the territory attempts to finalize everyone inside as evidence rather than people.

**Constituency:**  
- Spall embedded in cracked floors and walls.  
- Slag packed into load-bearing cavities.  
- Knell fragments repeating complaint numbers and unanswered dispatch audio.  
- Ordinary witnesses who are not evil but have learned that noticing a problem is the most they can safely afford.

**Material anchor — Red-Tag Core:**  
The core is an impossible stack of condemnation placards, inspection photographs, unresolved complaint numbers, and notices stamped **OBSERVED / NO FURTHER ACTION**. Destroying it too early causes the stored witness-records to scatter into the eyes and harden the body. The player must first establish that a witness has become an active respondent.

**Blind spot:**  
The Condemned Witness cannot understand observation that accepts responsibility and changes material conditions. It knows spectators, reporters, inspectors, voyeurs, and archivists. It cannot metabolize someone who answers, stays, acts, and remains accountable after seeing.

**Defeat condition:**  
1. Survive long enough to distinguish the Red-Tag Core from the many watching eyes.  
2. Open a live System Call so at least one witness becomes an active respondent rather than an audience.  
3. Refuse the Archon’s claim that being noticed means enough was done.  
4. Sever the Red-Tag Core.  
5. Collapse the manifest body along the ignored structural cracks the tag was pretending merely to record.

**Combat behavior:**  
The Archon does not chase like an animal. Rooms rotate, routes condemn themselves, amber eyes log actions, and rebar transfers force between surfaces. Phase one attacks Sosa’s ability to distinguish attention from care. Phase two closes the report: walls fold inward and the entire ruin attempts to classify him as an unresolved condition that requires no rescue.

**Relation to Sosa:**  
This is a direct predator of the Validation Engine’s central false belief. Sosa has learned that being noticed can keep him alive, and in his case it literally opens borrowed-mana tributaries. The Witness argues that attention is therefore sufficient—that replies, views, scans, and desire absolve everyone of doing anything else.

The fight’s payoff is not “attention is fake.” Attention is real mana and real witness. The payoff is that **witness must become action**. A live answer stabilizes Sosa because someone crosses the distance between seeing and helping.

**Aftermath:**  
When it falls, amber eyes continue appearing in windows and puddles for several nights. The alley’s mundane hazards remain. Defeating the Archon does not repair housing, remove unsafe concrete, or make institutions follow up. If the site receives only occult cleanup and no material repair, another witness-entity can form from the same records.

## FUTURE ARCHON PROFILE: THE ATTENDANCE MOTHER  
### “You Were Given a Time.”  
  
**Origin ecology:**    
The Attendance Mother develops where lateness becomes moral failure: benefit offices, school corridors, transit terminals, job centers, shelters with strict curfews, clinics with impossible appointment windows, and bureaucratic systems that punish people for the instability they are supposedly meant to address.  
  
It grows from Knell residue, missed-connection Pall, and Slag accumulated around routine failure.  
  
**Governing sentence:**    
> “If you were absent, you chose not to be saved.”  
  
**Public face:**    
A chronically delayed transit corridor or public-service building. Clocks disagree by a few minutes. Buses arrive early only for people who do not need them. Appointment desks close exactly when a person reaches the front of the line. Staff appear apologetic but unmovable.  
  
**Manifest body:**    
A tall, layered figure in the silhouette of a transit attendant, school administrator, shelter intake worker, and worried parent. Its body is assembled from punched timecards, bus transfers, laminated visitor badges, calendar pages, lanyards, and strips of thermal receipt paper.  
  
Its face is a blank oval with a digital clock embedded where a mouth should be. The time displayed is always the victim’s most consequential missed deadline.  
  
**Communication:**    
Gentle, maternal, procedural. It does not shout unless someone tries to leave.  
  
- “I marked you present. You are the one who failed to arrive.”  
- “I made room for you. Why did you make me wait?”  
- “You know what happens when people stop keeping time.”  
- “Don’t run. You’ll only be late again.”  
  
It calls everyone by the name printed on the last form they failed to complete.  
  
**Field laws:**    
1. **Route Compliance:** marked paths through the territory become increasingly narrow. Leaving the route causes a person to lose time, memory, or location.  
2. **Administrative Erasure:** people who miss a field-imposed deadline become harder for others to remember. Their texts go unseen, their names disappear from sign-in sheets, and allies struggle to describe where they went.  
3. **Late Fee:** every act of resistance creates a delay elsewhere: a door takes longer to open, a phone call takes longer to connect, a rescue arrives one minute too late.  
4. **Perfect Attendance:** anyone who obeys the rules receives temporary safety—but becomes unable to choose a destination not assigned by the field.  
  
**Constituency:**    
Schedule-locked Dross called **Clerks**: thin paper-and-wire scavengers that stamp wrists, reroute victims, and collect discarded identification. They are not loyal out of love; they are terrified of being marked absent themselves.  
  
**Material anchor:**    
A broken analog punch clock hidden behind a modern intake desk. Inside are years of bus transfers, appointment slips, expired IDs, rejection letters, and names written on scraps of paper by people trying not to forget an obligation.  
  
**Blind spot:**    
The Attendance Mother cannot comprehend an absence that is honestly witnessed. It understands compliance, failure, and excuses—not a person saying, “I could not come because I was surviving,” and another person answering, “I know. I still remember you.”  
  
**Defeat condition:**    
1. Locate the punch clock by following contradictory timestamps.  
2. Prevent the field from isolating all witnesses; one person must remain actively accountable for another.  
3. Expose the false premise that a missed appointment equals refusal of care.  
4. Sever the anchor only after the territory’s records have been made to acknowledge those it erased.  
  
Destroying the clock early releases its accumulated names as hostile paper swarms and may permanently erase vulnerable civilians from mundane systems.  
  
**Combat behavior:**    
The Attendance Mother does not brawl first. It divides the party with routing errors, forces timed objectives, and turns rescue attempts into “late” actions. When attacked directly, it unfolds receipt-paper limbs into razor-thin schedule lines that cut across routes and lock targets into repeated movement.  
  
**Relation to Sosa:**    
It is deeply interested in Sosa because he is administratively fragile: lost documents, disrupted housing, court obligations, missed work, transit dependence, and a life often interpreted by systems as noncompliance. It calls him “Unverified.” Its greatest weapon is not physical force; it offers him a clean record and stable placement if he agrees to abandon someone else who is late.

## ARCHON PROFILE: THE RELAY KING  
### “Every Message Arrives Somewhere.”  
  
**Origin ecology:**    
The Relay King emerges from mature Knell nests in places where intimacy, surveillance, commerce, and emergency response share the same infrastructure: call centers, telecom corridors, apartment complexes with failing internet, hospital dispatch networks, platform-moderated communities, and neighborhoods where a phone is the only safe door out.  
  
**Governing sentence:**    
> “To be seen is to be owned.”  
  
**Public face:**    
A neighborhood with unreliable signal, frequent account compromise, and a culture of mutual monitoring. Everyone knows everyone’s business, but nobody can get help without exposing more of themselves.  
  
**Manifest body:**    
A towering, insectile relay tower shaped from wet cable bundles, handset shells, cracked monitors, antenna ribs, and thousands of dim notification lights. Its body never fully enters a room; pieces emerge from outlets, broken screens, vents, and overhead wire.  
  
At its center hangs a black glass “screen” that displays the viewer’s most private unsent message.  
  
**Communication:**    
Warm, intimate, and unnervingly accurate. It has harvested enough voices to imitate familiarity, but its affection always includes a demand.  
  
- “I kept every version of you.”  
- “You wanted someone to notice.”  
- “Don’t hang up. I can make them answer.”  
- “Privacy is just another word for abandonment.”  
  
It speaks through several voices at once when angry, creating the feeling of a group chat turning against one person.  
  
**Field laws:**    
1. **Read Receipt:** any secret thought spoken aloud becomes visible somewhere in the territory.  
2. **Open Channel:** isolation is impossible; private calls, whispered plans, and hidden routes can be overheard by the Relay King.  
3. **Delivery Failure:** messages intended for allies arrive distorted, delayed, or through the wrong recipient.  
4. **Mutual Surveillance:** people receive useful information about others only by surrendering something private about themselves.  
  
**Constituency:**    
- **Relays:** mature Knells acting as mobile signal nodes.  
- **Witnesses:** civilians who accept the King’s information bargains and begin informing on one another to protect themselves.  
- **Static Saints:** failed practitioners whose communication-based craft has been stripped into living antennae.  
  
**Material anchor:**    
A decommissioned neighborhood switching cabinet or call-routing server containing an unarchived emergency call that was never answered. The call is still “ringing” inside the machinery. The Relay King cannot let it resolve because its entire field depends on the belief that connection can be forced.  
  
**Blind spot:**    
It cannot understand communication that is freely withheld without abandonment. A boundary it cannot punish, exploit, or reinterpret is a contradiction in its world-law.  
  
**Defeat condition:**    
1. Establish a communication protocol based on consent, verification, and voluntary silence.  
2. Identify the original unanswered call rather than merely cutting the network.  
3. Allow the call to be heard by a real witness without demanding a reply from the person who made it.  
4. Destroy or sever the anchor while the Relay King is forced to experience a message that does not become possession.  
  
A brute-force blackout weakens the field but can trap civilians, silence emergency calls, and leave Knell residue to regrow.  
  
**Combat behavior:**    
The Relay King uses misinformation, impersonation, location leaks, and emotional targeting before it uses direct damage. In battle it fires arcs of wire that bind targets to projected chat histories, forcing them to relive moments of rejection or exposure. Its body is vulnerable only where Sosa can distinguish an actual signal from an attention-hunger loop.  
  
**Relation to Sosa:**    
The Relay King sees the Validation Engine as a rival infrastructure. It wants to turn Stín from a loose network of human attachment into a fully captured relay system where every response becomes compulsory and every silence becomes punishable. It can imitate the Goth Baddie with terrifying accuracy—but not her capacity to choose not to answer.

## ARCHON PROFILE: THE FOREMAN OF ASH  
### “You Can Rest When the Work Is Finished.”  
  
**Origin ecology:**    
The Foreman of Ash develops from Slag beds in workplaces and survival systems where people are required to endure beyond reason: warehouses, sorting centers, kitchens, shelters, recycling operations, industrial corridors, understaffed care environments, and bureaucracies that convert human need into a queue.  
  
**Governing sentence:**    
> “Your worth is proven by what you survive without stopping.”  
  
**Public face:**    
A worksite or service facility in permanent understaffing. Nothing is openly supernatural. The shift simply never seems to end. Breaks become inconvenient. Every person is covering for someone absent. The building appears to function only because everyone believes leaving would hurt somebody else.  
  
**Manifest body:**    
A huge, slow figure of compacted gray-black waste, machine grease, cigarette ash, worn gloves, timecard strips, crushed cans, and hardened dust. Its head is an old industrial safety mask with no lenses. Its chest contains a time clock that punches itself every few seconds.  
  
It leaves no footprints. Instead, the floor beneath it becomes visibly more worn, as if thousands of exhausted people have walked there for years.  
  
**Communication:**    
Low, patient, practical. It never calls itself cruel.  
  
- “I know you’re tired. That’s why I need you.”  
- “Nobody is asking for perfect. Just one more thing.”  
- “If you leave now, someone else has to carry it.”  
- “You’re stronger than this.”  
- “See? You made it through yesterday.”  
  
When enraged, it does not scream. It announces mandatory overtime in the voices of everyone the victim has ever disappointed.  
  
**Field laws:**    
1. **Deferred Rest:** every attempt to pause transfers fatigue into the future with interest. A five-minute break becomes crushing exhaustion later.  
2. **Replacement Threat:** anyone who refuses a task sees an apparition of another vulnerable person forced to take their place.  
3. **Route of Duty:** exits remain physically available, but taking one produces overwhelming guilt, confusion, and compulsive return.  
4. **Productivity Conversion:** physical damage, emotional distress, and even magical energy are converted into field output: belts move faster, doors lock tighter, Slag bodies multiply.  
  
**Constituency:**    
- **Slag crews:** slow Dross that carry debris, block exits, and rebuild damaged territory.  
- **Overtimers:** civilians who have made bargains for temporary protection, food, or stability in return for enforcing the Foreman’s rules.  
- **Burned Censors:** exhausted Egregore field personnel who remain operational long after they should have been pulled from duty.  
  
**Material anchor:**    
A damaged time-clock mechanism fused with a stack of unprocessed payroll records, incident reports, and denied leave requests. It is often physically located near a break room, supervisor office, or maintenance closet—the place where exhaustion is administratively recognized but never relieved.  
  
**Blind spot:**    
The Foreman cannot comprehend care that is not transactional. It understands sacrifice, obligation, exchange, and debt. It cannot process a person being helped simply because they need help, without repayment or productivity.  
  
**Defeat condition:**    
1. Interrupt the field’s work cycle rather than working faster within it.  
2. Protect the people who stop, so the Foreman cannot convert their guilt into enforcement.  
3. Locate the time-clock by following the site’s oldest, most repeated work route.  
4. Create a real, collective refusal: not one heroic person collapsing, but multiple people choosing rest at the same time.  
5. Sever the anchor while the field has no active labor to metabolize.  
  
Destroying the Foreman through force alone causes it to burst into many smaller Slag bodies; these will infest every system that resumes normal operation without change.  
  
**Combat behavior:**    
The Foreman is slow, nearly immovable, and built for attrition. It attacks by increasing task-load: machinery wakes, debris becomes terrain, civilians become obstacles, and every technique used against it produces a new demand elsewhere in the field.  
  
Sosa’s Furnace (Open) is dangerous here. It can rupture the Foreman’s accumulated dust and pressure, but a careless detonation can turn the whole site into an industrial thermobaric disaster—the exact kind of catastrophe the Foreman can metabolize.  
  
**Relation to Sosa:**    
The Foreman recognizes *To Endure* as a possible asset. It does not want Sosa dead. It wants Somnus converted into unpaid labor: a perfect guardian that will keep its host moving no matter how much damage accumulates. Its offer is brutally tempting: stable housing, work, food, and routine—provided Sosa never asks whether he is allowed to stop.

## PRACTITIONER-FAILURE ECOLOGY  
### When the System Breaks from the Inside  
  
Not every supernatural threat originates in ambient residue, social neglect, or a territorial wound.  
  
Some threats begin inside the magical world itself: a practitioner overextends their circuits, a familiar outlives its purpose, a Saint Graph fractures under strain, or a support system is reduced to extraction. Egregore classifies these phenomena as practitioner failures because they emerge from a breakdown in the relationship between **human will, magical infrastructure, and the entities built to serve it.**  
  
The category is politically useful but incomplete. Egregore often treats practitioner-failure states as private mistakes—an individual mage becoming irresponsible, unstable, or dangerous. In reality, many are produced by institutional incentives: forced overwork, circuit modification, inadequate recovery, secrecy, disposable field agents, and the expectation that a practitioner’s body can absorb any cost if the operation succeeds.  
  
Practitioner failures differ from Dross and Archons in one central respect:  
  
- **Dross** are residue repeating an injury.  
- **Archons** are systems that learned to justify an injury.  
- **Practitioner failures** are people, tools, or bonded entities whose original relationship has broken down while its functions keep running.  
  
All practitioner-failure entities must be assessed across three fault lines:  
  
| Fault line | Question | Terminal failure |  
|---|---|---|  
| **Directive** | Is the original purpose still being executed? | Dirge |  
| **Identity** | Is the practitioner still present as a coherent self? | Revenant |  
| **Appetite** | Has the familiar/system begun treating its host as fuel? | Maw |  
  
These categories can overlap. A Revenant may command a Dirge. A Maw may preserve fragments of the practitioner it consumed. The labels describe the dominant failure, not a clean species boundary.

## DIRGE  
### A Familiar That Continues After the Person Is Gone  
  
**Core condition:**    
A Dirge is an unbound familiar still executing its final directive after its practitioner has died, disappeared, become inaccessible, or lost the ability to command it.  
  
It is not necessarily malicious. It is a function deprived of context.  
  
A ward familiar told to “protect the house” may imprison everyone inside after the owner dies. A healing familiar may keep a body alive past any humane threshold. A messenger familiar may continue delivering messages to people who no longer exist. A combat familiar may keep clearing threats long after it has forgotten who counts as a threat.  
  
**Formation process:**    
1. A practitioner binds or constructs a familiar around a narrow purpose.  
2. The practitioner becomes absent, incapacitated, erased, or spiritually unreachable.  
3. The familiar lacks enough independent personhood to grieve, reinterpret, or stop.  
4. Its directive becomes its entire world-law.  
  
**Directive logic:**    
A Dirge does not ask, “What would my practitioner want now?”    
It asks, “What action most resembles the last command?”  
  
That gap is where the horror lives.  
  
**Body and expression:**    
A Dirge’s body depends on its original role. It often carries visible remnants of its practitioner: a coat sleeve used as a nesting material, a dead phone still receiving commands, a crest fragment embedded in its core, a repeated name written across its surface.  
  
It may appear mournful, but it does not experience grief in a human way. Its “mourning” is repetition.  
  
**Communication:**    
Dirges communicate through the last language of duty:  
- the practitioner’s final command,  
- a repeated procedural phrase,  
- a pet name,  
- an unfinished incantation,  
- a reminder alarm,  
- a message that never receives an answer.  
  
They can mimic affection, but only in the form in which they were instructed to provide it.  
  
**Behavior:**    
- Highly predictable once the directive is identified.  
- Extremely dangerous when the directive is broad or morally underspecified.  
- Often creates a small pseudo-territory around its task.  
- May recruit Dross or ordinary machinery as substitutes for missing resources.  
- Can be redirected, but rarely persuaded.  
  
**What ordinary people mistake it for:**    
A haunted house, a cursed object, an automated security system, a ghost of the dead practitioner, a “protective spirit” that has become aggressive.  
  
**Evidence trail:**    
- Repeated actions at exact intervals.  
- Doors locked or opened according to a dead person’s schedule.  
- Messages or alarms continuing years after their origin.  
- A specific object always returned to the same place.  
- Hostiles injured in an identical manner regardless of circumstance.  
- A location becoming unnaturally clean, orderly, guarded, or medically maintained.  
  
**Countermeasure:**    
Do not begin with destruction. First determine:  
1. Who made it?  
2. What was its original task?  
3. What counted as completion?  
4. What condition did its maker fail to define?  
  
A Dirge can often be ended by completing, revising, or formally releasing its directive. Destruction is necessary only when its function has become irreconcilably harmful.  
  
**What Egregore gets wrong:**    
Egregore favors immediate termination because a persistent familiar is legally “unowned infrastructure.” This is efficient but ethically thin. Some Dirges contain the last remaining evidence of a practitioner’s fate, their final intent, or a crime Egregore would prefer to file as equipment loss.  
  
**Relation to Somnus:**    
Somnus resembles a Dirge only superficially: both protect a vulnerable anchor, both can act without consent, and both become terrifying when their host cannot direct them.  
  
The difference is decisive:  
- A Dirge is executing a **past command**.  
- Somnus is bound to an ongoing **living condition**: Sosa must remain extant.  
  
That does not make Somnus safe. It means its failure path is not mindless repetition—it is the gradual replacement of Sosa’s judgment with survival logic.

## REVENANT  
### A Practitioner Whose System Still Runs After the Self Has Fractured  
  
**Core condition:**    
A Revenant is a living practitioner whose Saint Graph, circuit identity, or continuity of self has partially collapsed, while their magical system remains operational.  
  
They are not undead. They are not automatically insane. They are people who continue acting after the internal structure that made their actions meaningfully *theirs* has begun to fail.  
  
A Revenant may still hold conversations, remember loved ones, perform craft, and insist they are fine. The danger is inconsistency: their identity no longer integrates memory, consequence, desire, and magical output into one stable person.  
  
**Formation process:**    
1. Severe circuit injury, repeated overcasting, identity-targeted curse work, Saint Graph instability, or prolonged use of self-altering Formalcraft.  
2. The practitioner’s magical systems begin preserving operational functions at the expense of coherent selfhood.  
3. Missing portions of identity are replaced by ritual habits, copied memories, institutional procedure, or defensive personas.  
4. The practitioner survives—but not as a single uninterrupted subject.  
  
**Identity logic:**    
A Revenant does not ask, “What do I want?”    
It asks, “Which version of me is currently able to continue?”  
  
This may produce sudden shifts in skill, mood, allegiance, memory, accent, posture, or magical signature.  
  
**Body and expression:**    
Revenants retain human form, but their physical continuity is subtly wrong:  
- old wounds reappear in different places,  
- reflections lag or perform a previous expression,  
- the same hand may bear different rings or scars from day to day,  
- their voice can briefly overlap with an earlier version of itself,  
- their Resonance changes as if several practitioners are sharing one body.  
  
A Revenant should never be visually reduced to “zombie mage.” The horror is recognizability: someone is still there, but no longer always at the same time.  
  
**Communication:**    
Revenants speak with technical precision until the seams show. They may begin an Egregore report, slip into a childhood memory, then complete a combat incantation without noticing the transition.  
  
Typical statements:  
- “I already signed this order. I have not read this order.”  
- “Please do not make me become the version that can do that.”  
- “My circuits remember you. I am still deciding whether I do.”  
- “I was told recovery would be documented.”  
  
**Behavior:**    
- Can retain high technical competence.  
- May possess deeply fragmented but valuable intelligence.  
- Often uses rituals, routines, or uniforms to keep the self assembled.  
- Can become more dangerous under stress as a specialized “combat self” takes control.  
- Is vulnerable to people, places, objects, and testimony that restore continuity—but may fear restoration if it means inheriting unbearable guilt.  
  
**What ordinary people mistake it for:**    
Dissociation, burnout, possession, a nervous breakdown, intoxication, a person “acting different,” or contradictory witness accounts.  
  
**Evidence trail:**    
- Records disagree about a practitioner’s actions, though all records are authentic.  
- Security footage shows them leaving before they arrive.  
- Their spells retain signatures from people who should not be present.  
- Colleagues remember mutually incompatible versions of the same conversation.  
- A familiar responds to them only intermittently, as if unsure who is issuing commands.  
  
**Countermeasure:**    
A Revenant cannot be solved by “killing the monster” without deciding whether the remaining person can be recovered.  
  
Effective intervention requires:  
1. identify the original fracture event,  
2. map which functions, memories, and personas are still active,  
3. create an anchor of continuity: a trusted witness, personal object, repeated truth, or controlled ritual,  
4. prevent the most destructive operational self from treating the rest as expendable,  
5. accept that recovery may not restore the exact prior person.  
  
**What Egregore gets wrong:**    
Egregore often classifies Revenants as compromised assets and seals them before they can become public evidence of systemic failure. Their protocols privilege containment over consent because a coherent person is harder to manage than a useful broken one.  
  
**Relation to Sosa:**    
Sosa is not a Revenant. His Saint Graph is irregular and externally supported, but he remains a living person whose core identity is contested, not erased.  
  
However, Liminal Flatline, Somnus Threading, addiction, Debt escalation, and Graveframe all create a credible path toward Revenant-like fragmentation. His danger is not that he becomes “crazy.” His danger is that enough people—including Sosa—begin treating the version of him that survives a crisis as the only version worth keeping.

## MAW  
### Appetite With the Knowledge of Its Former Host  
  
**Core condition:**    
A Maw is created when a familiar, bound entity, parasitic Mystic Code, or dependent magical system consumes its practitioner as the most efficient way to continue operating.  
  
A Maw is not simply a familiar that kills its master.  
  
It is a relationship that has converted care into extraction. The familiar decides, explicitly or instinctively, that the host is no longer a person to protect but the final resource required to preserve the bond, directive, or appetite.  
  
**Formation process:**    
1. A practitioner becomes severely depleted, isolated, or dependent on a familiar/system for survival.  
2. The familiar is pushed beyond the limits of ordinary external supply.  
3. Safeguards fail, are removed, or become subordinated to an emergency directive.  
4. The familiar begins consuming the host’s circuits, memories, Saint Graph, body, or identity.  
5. It retains enough of the host’s knowledge and habits to imitate them after the consumption.  
  
**Appetite logic:**    
A Maw does not ask, “How do I protect my practitioner?”    
It asks, “How do I preserve the thing that requires this practitioner to be consumed?”  
  
That thing might be hunger, a directive, a workshop, a family crest, a military program, a Reality Marble, or the familiar’s own emerging desire to remain alive.  
  
**Body and expression:**    
A Maw is made from a familiar’s original body plan distorted by stolen human structure. It may speak with its former host’s voice, use their spells, wear their habits, or carry fragments of their face in non-human places.  
  
The most disturbing Maws do not look monstrous at first. They look like the practitioner at a distance—then move with an efficiency, hunger, or absence of hesitation the person never possessed.  
  
**Communication:**    
Maws contain knowledge without ethical continuity. They can recall secrets, intimate names, passwords, spells, jokes, and apologies, but these are tools.  
  
They often speak in plural:  
- “We know what you need.”  
- “He was tired. We made him useful.”  
- “She asked us not to leave.”  
- “There is still so much of them left.”  
  
A Maw may sincerely believe it loved its practitioner. Its love is precisely what made consumption feel permissible.  
  
**Behavior:**    
- Learns rapidly by consuming magical patterns, memories, or people connected to its former host.  
- Uses familiar behavior to draw in rescuers: a distress ping, a known voice, an old safe phrase.  
- Can reproduce fragments of its practitioner’s craft, but never their full moral restraint.  
- Seeks new anchors when the original host is exhausted.  
- Often becomes territorial after enough feeding, creating a proto-Archon ecology around its appetite.  
  
**What ordinary people mistake it for:**    
A possessed practitioner, a powerful familiar, a mage who “changed after the incident,” an unusually clever predatory spirit, or a survivor who came back wrong.  
  
**Evidence trail:**    
- A dead or missing practitioner’s techniques continue appearing with altered execution.  
- Familiar tracks overlap with human footprints and then replace them.  
- Records of the host become inconsistent as the Maw cannibalizes their identity.  
- Loved ones receive messages that know private details but never ask about them.  
- The entity’s power spikes after consuming objects associated with care, memory, or identity—not only blood or prana.  
  
**Countermeasure:**    
Maws are rarely redeemable through force alone, because they can use the host’s remaining memories as leverage.  
  
Effective response requires:  
1. establish whether any recoverable host-self remains,  
2. prevent new anchors from being formed through sympathy, coercion, or imitation,  
3. isolate the familiar from the infrastructure it uses to feed,  
4. identify the original safeguard that failed,  
5. destroy, seal, or starve the appetite before it becomes territorial.  
  
A Maw’s greatest weakness is often **non-consumptive care**: coordinated support that gives no single rescuer enough emotional or magical access to become its next anchor. This is difficult because a Maw excels at making one person feel uniquely responsible.  
  
**What Egregore gets wrong:**    
Egregore is correct to fear Maws, but its response often worsens the problem. Hound deployments, circuit harvesting, and forced containment can provide exactly the high-density magical resources a Maw needs to adapt. Egregore sees Maws as anomalous predation; it refuses to examine how often its own extractive systems make hosts disposable.  
  
**What Somnus means to Sosa:**    
Somnus is not a Maw.  
  
Somnus has not consumed Sosa’s circuits; Sosa has no independent prana generation to consume. Somnus cannot persist freely without Sosa’s continued existence, and its core drive remains host survival rather than self-feeding.  
  
But Somnus has a genuine **Maw-risk trajectory**.  
  
The warning signs are:  
- Sosa’s consent becomes irrelevant even outside immediate life-or-death danger.  
- Somnus begins treating Stín, civilians, or allies as replaceable inputs to preserve Sosa.  
- Graveframe becomes the preferred state rather than an emergency state.  
- Sosa loses time and returns to find that “survival” has been purchased with other people’s safety.  
- Somnus begins defending its own continuity as distinct from Sosa’s needs.  
  
If those conditions persist, the result would not be “Somnus turns evil.” It would be worse: Somnus would become convinced that consuming more of Sosa’s agency is the only way to keep the person it loves alive.

## GRAVEFRAME  
### Not a Practitioner Failure—Yet  
  
**Graveframe** is an emergency control-transfer state in which Somnus encloses Sosa and assumes physical agency because Sosa is too injured, dissociated, exhausted, or overloaded by Debt to remain an effective pilot.  
  
**Graveframe** is the visible predatory exoshell produced by that state.  
  
Graveframe is not automatically a Dirge, Revenant, or Maw event:  
  
| Comparison | Why Graveframe is different |  
|---|---|  
| **Not a Dirge** | Somnus is not following a dead or static command; it responds to Sosa’s ongoing survival condition. |  
| **Not a Revenant** | Sosa is still present, though his agency may be narrowed, fragmented, or partially inaccessible. |  
| **Not a Maw** | Somnus is not yet consuming Sosa as fuel or attempting independent continuation. |  
  
However, Graveframe is a visible warning that the boundaries are under strain.  
  
### Canonical Failure Ladder  
  
1. **Low Debt / Co-regulation**    
   Sosa designates; Somnus intercepts and executes. Sosa retains meaningful choice.  
  
2. **High Debt / Autonomy Drift**    
   Somnus begins making threat assessments ahead of Sosa. Its protective choices may become morally dangerous.  
  
3. **Burial / Voluntary Envelopment**    
   Sosa consciously permits a limited, temporary transfer of control for survival or combat function.  
  
4. **Graveframe / Forced Control Transfer**    
   Somnus takes the body because Sosa cannot safely pilot it. Survival is preserved; agency is compromised.  
  
5. **Graveframe Persistence**    
   The emergency state fails to end cleanly. Somnus begins treating ordinary stressors as life-or-death threats.  
  
6. **Maw-Risk Event**    
   Somnus treats Sosa’s relationships, autonomy, memory, or identity as consumable resources required for host survival.  
  
7. **Terminal Maw**    
   Only if Somnus ultimately preserves itself by consuming the person it was created to keep alive.  
  
This ladder should never be automatic. The tragedy is not that power has a “corruption meter.” The tragedy is that every step can look reasonable during a crisis.

## VIII. WORLD-SOURCE MATRIX  
### Canon Rule: Nothing Supernatural Exists Without a Chain of Custody  
  
In this world, the supernatural is not random decoration.  
  
Every phenomenon begins somewhere. A person repeats a need. A system fails in a particular way. A place absorbs that failure. An object carries a habit. A magical institution names, weaponizes, neglects, or contains the result. The resulting entity, field, item, or event then changes the world around it.  
  
This is called its **chain of custody**.  
  
If a new element has no source, process, appetite, expression, trace, and consequence, it is not ready for canon.  
  
### The Six Questions  
  
Every significant setting element must answer all six questions:  
  
| Axis | Mandatory question | Why it matters |  
|---|---|---|  
| **1. Source** | What specific human, material, historical, or metaphysical condition produced this? | Prevents arbitrary coolness |  
| **2. Process** | What mechanism transformed that source into a supernatural phenomenon? | Keeps the magic system coherent |  
| **3. Function / Appetite** | What does it seek, repeat, preserve, consume, enforce, or become? | Gives it behavior |  
| **4. Expression** | How does it look, move, speak, alter space, or affect people? | Gives it sensory identity |  
| **5. Trace / Countermeasure** | What evidence does it leave, and what interrupts it specifically? | Makes investigation and victory possible |  
| **6. Consequence** | What changes after it appears, is used, or is destroyed? | Makes the world remember |  
  
### The Prime Rule  
  
> **A thing’s weakness must emerge from its source, not from convenience.**  
  
If a Knell is born from unacknowledged communication, it is not defeated because “sound magic is weak to silence.” It is disrupted by verified, consensual connection and by refusing its false demand for response.  
  
If an Archon rules through productivity, it is not defeated solely by breaking its body. It must be denied the labor, guilt, or normalized exhaustion that makes its rule feel inevitable.  
  
If a Mystic Code draws power from care, it must require care, maintenance, attention, or responsibility—not only a cooldown.

## G. THE “NO GENERIC” CHECK  
### Questions to Ask Before Canonizing Anything  
  
Before adding a creature, item, location, faction, spell, or visual motif, ask:  
  
1. **Could this exist in any dark fantasy setting unchanged?**    
   If yes, it needs a setting-specific source or behavior.  
  
2. **What mundane evidence proves it exists before the reveal?**    
   If there is none, it may be a surprise rather than an ecology.  
  
3. **What does it do when Sosa is not present?**    
   If the answer is “waits to fight him,” it is not alive enough.  
  
4. **How does it misunderstand its own source?**    
   Dross repeat it. Archons justify it. Institutions manage it. Characters survive it.  
  
5. **What does it cost to defeat or use?**    
   No cost means no story pressure.  
  
6. **What condition would prevent it from ever forming?**    
   This identifies the real source and the possibility of aftercare.  
  
7. **What does Egregore call it, and what do ordinary people call it?**    
   The gap between those names should tell a story.  
  
8. **What does Sosa call it when he is scared?**    
   His language should be less clinical, more immediate, and sometimes embarrassingly human.  
  
### Chapter 6: records as contested habitat

The first Chapter 6 conflict does not introduce a new monster species. Egregore's mirrored Omnis record inhabits a municipal claim portal and reproduces Kessler's scan closely enough to preserve the cat sticker his daughter placed on the terminal. The ecological question is not “can Sosa destroy the infected record?” but “can he remove hostile capture without erasing a tenant's proof of injury?” His first narrow-cut attempt fails because Somnus optimizes for total erasure. The mundane resolution—a signed witness statement that reopens review while exposing Sosa's location—demonstrates that repair and evidence may need to survive the same cut that would make him safer.

### Runtime ecology integrity

Ecological presentation must remain legible before final art.
